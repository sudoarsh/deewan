Codicological Survey

Establishing the Hierarchy of Truth in the Deccani Tradition

The Core Academic Thesis

For over 120 years, modern scholarship has misread Mah Laqa Bai Chanda’s poetic evolution, assuming textual variations were the result of artistic instability. By conducting a forensic word-level collation, this edition proves her original authorial voice was remarkably stable. The heavy variations and linguistic normalizations previously attributed to her are actually late textual corruptions introduced by an anonymous 1906 print editor, effectively erasing her 18th-century Delhi-Deccani synthesis to fit colonial-era standard Urdu.

Salar Jung (1818) SJ

Role: Matn-e-Asas (The Base Text)

Compiled in the final decade of her life, this manuscript represents her final authorial intention. It exhibits highly stable orthography, deliberate structural curation, and mature aesthetic choices. It serves as the primary anchor line for our structural alignment.

British Library (1799) BL

Role: Primary Genetic Witness

Compiled during her youth under immediate courtly supervision. It is an invaluable document for tracking early authorial variants (Ikhtilaf-e-Nuskha) and serves as the source for repairing physical lacunae in SJ. Crucially, it preserves the original Persian Dibacha detailing state patronage, which was scrubbed from later prints.

Telangana Archives (1811) TO

Role: Intermediate Witness

A crucial chronological bridge between her early courtly output and her final compiled works, allowing us to accurately date when specific verses were added, removed, or revised.

Print Edition (1906) GM

Role: The Corrupted Tradition

Published anonymously in Hyderabad. This witness is isolated to document textual distortion. The 1906 editor systematically modernized her vocabulary, stripped Deccani inflections, and restructured entire lines to fit early 20th-century standard grammar of the Delhi and Lucknow schools.
